2. PRONUNCIATION – Short and long vowel sounds

Now, pronunciation time! English vowels can be short or long. For example, ship and sheep. Can you hear the difference?

Ship – barco; sheep – oveja. The first sound is short, the second one is long. Let’s try more:
  • sit – sentarse, seat – asiento
  • bit – un poco, beat – golpear

Remember: when a sound is long, we hold it a bit more. Practice helps your ear get used to English rhythm.

3. LISTENING & SPEAKING – Your food profile

Let’s move to a listening activity. Imagine we’re interviewing people about their food habits. Listen to this text carefully.

“My name’s Emma. I usually have breakfast at 8:00 a.m. I love porridge with fruit. I have lunch at work between 12:30 and 1:00 p.m., usually a salad or sandwich. For dinner, I prefer cooking pasta or vegetables. I try not to eat late because it affects my sleep. I drink coffee twice a day, never after 3:00 p.m., and I love green tea in the evening.”

Nice! Emma’s routine sounds healthy. Now, talk about yourself. When do you usually eat breakfast? What do you have for lunch or dinner?

For example:
  • I have breakfast at seven thirty – Desayuno a las siete y media
  • I don’t drink coffee after lunch – No tomo café después del almuerzo

Remember to use present simple for routines: I eat, I drink, I cook. But when talking about what’s happening now, we use present continuous: I’m drinking coffee right now – Estoy tomando café ahora mismo.

Great distinction! Let’s keep exploring how time affects the way we eat, and why eating at the right time matters.

4. READING & LISTENING – Eat and drink… but at the right time!

Now, let’s read something really interesting. Scientists say that when we eat is just as important as what we eat. Let’s listen to this article carefully.

In our busy 21st-century lives, most of us try to do as much as we can in 24 hours. However, using time as we want might disrupt our body’s natural rhythm. Science shows that by doing things at the right time, we can be happier and healthier.

Have breakfast at 8:00 a.m.
In recent studies, researchers found that having breakfast at this time is good for blood sugar levels. The food we eat now will give us energy for the day. It’s also important not to skip breakfast. Another research team suggested that people who eat regular breakfasts as teenagers are less likely to be at risk of heart disease as adults.

Don’t drink coffee in the morning. Have one at 3:00 p.m.
Most coffee drinkers make a cup as soon as they wake up. But scientists say that early morning is the worst time to drink coffee because it stops our body from producing cortisol, the hormone that helps us wake up naturally. The best time for coffee is between 2:00 p.m. and 5:00 p.m., when it makes us more alert.

Have lunch between 12:00 and 2:00 p.m.
This is when the body digests food best. Studies show that people who eat their main meal in the middle of the day have lower blood sugar levels and more energy.

Have dinner at 7:00 p.m.
Dinner should be early and light. Eating late at night can cause weight gain and disturb sleep.

That’s fascinating! So, eating at the right time helps our body work better.

Exactly. Let’s check comprehension with some discussion questions.

Great reflection! This shows how our habits affect our health. Remember: Healthy eating is not only about what you eat, but also when you eat.

5. GRAMMAR – Present simple and continuous

Now let’s look at some grammar that helps us talk about our daily habits and current actions: present simple and present continuous.

We use present simple for routines, facts, and habits. For example:
  • I eat breakfast at eight – Desayuno a las ocho
  • She works in a restaurant – Ella trabaja en un restaurante
  • They don’t drink coffee – Ellos no toman café

We use present continuous for actions happening right now or temporary situations. For example:
  • I’m cooking dinner – Estoy cocinando la cena
  • She’s drinking tea – Ella está tomando té
  • We’re learning about food – Estamos aprendiendo sobre comida

Remember the structure:
Present simple: subject + base verb (+ s/es for he, she, it).
Present continuous: subject + am/is/are + verb + -ing.

Let’s compare:
  • I eat at home every day – Como en casa todos los días
  • I’m eating at a restaurant now – Estoy comiendo en un restaurante ahora

That’s a perfect example! The first describes a routine, the second describes what’s happening now.

Try saying one sentence with the present simple and one with the continuous about your life. For instance:
  • I work in an office – Trabajo en una oficina
  • I’m working from home this week – Estoy trabajando desde casa esta semana

Excellent! Keep practicing both tenses to describe your habits and current activities naturally.

Marianna Leivaditaki is the head chef of Morito, a popular restaurant on Hackney Road in London. She was born on the island of Crete, in Greece. Her father was a fisherman and her mother was Scottish. Together, they ran a small seafood restaurant where Marianna spent most of her childhood.

“We ate fish every day,” she says, “which we boiled, fried, or grilled. I wrote down recipes I wanted to cook for my family. My aunt, Thía Koula, had goats and made cheese, and my mother sold it in the restaurant. Occasionally, my mum used to buy me souvlaki for lunch — a sort of Greek sandwich with meat and salad. It was my favorite treat.”

What a beautiful story! Marianna’s childhood sounds full of flavor and love.

Absolutely. Let’s talk about it. What did Marianna’s parents do?

They ran a seafood restaurant in Crete.

Right. And what’s the name of her restaurant in London?

Morito. She’s the head chef there.

Exactly. Notice how we use past simple to talk about her childhood:
  • She wrote down recipes – Ella anotaba recetas

And we use present simple to talk about what’s true now:
  • She works as a chef – Ella trabaja como chef

Do you ever cook dinner at home? – ¿Cenas en casa alguna vez?

Do you prefer eating at home or eating out? – ¿Prefieres comer en casa o fuera?

Are you eating healthy these days? – ¿Estás comiendo saludable estos días?

I’m trying to! I’m eating more vegetables and drinking less soda.

Excellent answer! Remember, when we talk about current habits that are temporary or in progress, we use present continuous, like I’m eating more vegetables.

That’s right. You can also say, I’m trying to eat less sugar – Estoy intentando comer menos azúcar.

Fantastic! You’re doing great practicing both tenses in real conversation.

10. WRITING & REFLECTION

Let’s close our lesson with a short writing reflection. Think of your own food profile. Write a short paragraph answering these questions:

– What do you usually eat for breakfast, lunch, and dinner?
– What are you eating more or less of these days?
– Do you prefer eating in or eating out? Why?

For example:
“I usually eat breakfast at 7:30 a.m. I have toast and orange juice. For lunch, I eat at the university canteen, and for dinner, I cook something light at home. These days, I’m eating more vegetables and less meat because I want to be healthier.”

Perfect example! Remember to mix present simple for your habits and present continuous for things happening now.
