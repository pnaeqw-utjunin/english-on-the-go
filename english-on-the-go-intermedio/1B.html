1. VOCABULARY & SPEAKING – Family

Welcome back to English in the Go! Today we’re talking about something we all have — family! Families come in many shapes and sizes, and today we’ll learn how to describe them in English.

Exactly! Let’s start with a few questions. What’s the difference between a father and a parent?

Well, a father – un padre is one person, but a parent – un padre o madre can mean either your mother or father.

Right! And what about a mother and a stepmother?

A stepmother – una madrastra is your father’s new wife, not your biological mother.

Great! Families today are more diverse than ever. Let’s read about the modern family in Britain. Listen carefully.

Family life is changing all over the world. It’s not just the structure of the family, but also its habits.
A recent survey of British families showed some surprising statistics.
60% of families have married parents.
22% are single-parent families, and most of the single parents are women.
3% of families have one child, 62% have two, and 15% have three or more.
11% of families have stepchildren.
On average, women get married at 34 and men at 37.

Family habits are also changing.
20% of families only eat together once or twice a week, and never at the same time.
They visit friends or extended family twice a month.
75% of people are happiest with their families, and 17% with their friends.
The most common reason for arguments is children refusing to put down their phones or tablets.

Wow! That’s very interesting. Only 20% of families eat together regularly — that’s really low!

Yes! It shows how modern life has changed family time. Now, let’s talk about our own families.

Sure! For example: I live with my parents and my younger sister – Vivo con mis padres y mi hermana menor.
We usually have dinner together every night – Normalmente cenamos juntos todas las noches.

That’s nice! In my case: I live alone, but I visit my parents every weekend – Vivo solo, pero visito a mis padres cada fin de semana.

Great! Now let’s expand vocabulary. Repeat after us:

Let’s use them in examples.

Very good! And remember, your immediate family – familia cercana includes parents, brothers, and sisters. Your extended family – familia extendida includes grandparents, uncles, aunts, and cousins.

3. LISTENING & GRAMMAR – Future forms

Now, let’s move to grammar. We’re going to learn how to talk about the future using present continuous and be going to.

Yes! We use these forms when we talk about plans or intentions. For example:
  • I’m visiting my grandparents next week – Voy a visitar a mis abuelos la próxima semana.
  • She’s going to start university soon – Ella va a empezar la universidad pronto.

Perfect! Notice how both express future plans that are already decided.

And here’s another: We’re having a big family dinner this Saturday – Vamos a tener una gran cena familiar este sábado.

4. GRAMMAR – Future forms: present continuous and be going to

Let’s continue exploring how to talk about the future. There are several ways in English, but today we’ll focus on be going to and present continuous for future plans.

Yes! We use be going to to talk about things we intend to do or have already decided.

For example:
  • I’m going to call my brother tonight – Voy a llamar a mi hermano esta noche.
  • She’s going to study abroad next year – Ella va a estudiar en el extranjero el próximo año.

And we use present continuous for fixed arrangements, something we’ve already organized.
  • I’m meeting my parents for lunch tomorrow – Me reuniré con mis padres para almorzar mañana.
  • We’re visiting our grandparents on Sunday – Visitaremos a nuestros abuelos el domingo.

Notice how both tenses talk about the future, but the feeling is slightly different.
Be going to = plan or intention.
Present continuous = confirmed arrangement.

Let’s try a quick mini-quiz! I’ll say a sentence, and you decide if it’s be going to or present continuous.
Number one: We’re having dinner with my uncle tonight.
That’s present continuous – it’s an arrangement.
Exactly! Number two: I’m going to buy a gift for my mom.
That’s be going to, because it’s a plan.

Well done! Keep practicing both forms to sound natural when you talk about the future.

5. PRONUNCIATION & SPEAKING – Sentence stress

Now, pronunciation time! In English, some words in a sentence are stressed, and others are not.

Yes. We usually stress content words — the ones that carry meaning: nouns, verbs, adjectives, and adverbs.

Let’s listen to a sentence: I’m going to look for a job – Voy a buscar trabajo.
Which words are stressed?

The stressed words are going, look, and job. We don’t stress I’m or to.

Exactly! Listen and repeat with the right rhythm:
I’m going to look for a job.
Now a few more:
  • We’re meeting our cousins tomorrow – Nos reuniremos con nuestros primos mañana.
  • She’s going to start a new course – Ella va a comenzar un nuevo curso.
  • They’re traveling abroad this month – Ellos viajarán al extranjero este mes.

Excellent rhythm! Paying attention to stress makes your English sound more natural and easier to understand.

Let’s have a quick conversation practice. Answer with your own ideas as we go along.

Nice! Are you going to travel abroad this year? – ¿Vas a viajar al extranjero este año?
Not this year, but I’m going to plan a trip for next summer.

Good idea! Now let’s use Do you think…? questions for predictions.

Do you think more couples will have just one child in the future? – ¿Crees que más parejas tendrán solo un hijo en el futuro?

Do you think young adults will live with their parents longer? – ¿Crees que los jóvenes vivirán con sus padres por más tiempo?
Yes, definitely! It’s common now in many countries.

Perfect! So, we use be going to and will to talk about what we believe or expect in the future.

Exactly. For example:
  • I think families will spend more time together – Creo que las familias pasarán más tiempo juntas.
  • People are going to work from home more often – La gente va a trabajar desde casa con más frecuencia.

Great job, everyone! You’re doing fantastic learning how to talk about family and future plans naturally. Next, we’ll explore how our position in the family — being the oldest, middle, or youngest child — can shape our personality.

Families don’t just shape our habits — they also shape our personalities! Let’s learn some adjectives to describe personality.

Yes! For example:
extroverted – extrovertido,

Great! Let’s use them in sentences.

  • She’s extroverted but her sister is shy – Ella es extrovertida pero su hermana es tímida.

Excellent! You can also describe yourself. For example:
  • I’m generous with my friends – Soy generoso con mis amigos.
  • I’m a bit impatient sometimes – Soy un poco impaciente a veces.

Remember, adjectives of personality are very useful when talking about family members, friends, or coworkers.

8. READING – How birth order influences your personality

Now let’s read something fascinating: how your birth order — whether you’re the oldest, middle, or youngest child — might influence your personality.

Nowadays, it’s accepted that our position in the family strongly affects who we are. Being an oldest child, a middle child, a youngest child, or an only child can shape our character and behavior.

The oldest child often looks after younger brothers and sisters. They’re usually sensible and responsible, and they like to be leaders. However, they can sometimes be anxious or perfectionist.

The middle child tends to be sociable and relaxed. They’re good at solving conflicts and often act as peacemakers. But sometimes they feel ignored and lack direction.

The youngest child is often outgoing and funny. They get a lot of attention from parents, so they can become charming but also a little spoiled.

The only child usually spends more time with adults. They’re independent, organized, and sometimes a bit selfish or impatient.

That’s an interesting theory! Do you think it’s true for your family?

I think so. I’m the oldest child, and I definitely like to organize things. I’m also a bit perfectionist.

That makes sense. I’m the youngest, and I admit I’m more relaxed — maybe too relaxed sometimes!

Let’s make it a small reflection. Think about your own family. Are you the oldest, middle, youngest, or only child? How does that affect your personality?

For example:
  • I’m the middle child, and I’m very sociable – Soy el hijo del medio y soy muy sociable.
  • I’m an only child, and I’m independent – Soy hijo único y soy independiente.

Wonderful! Remember, there’s no “right” or “wrong” personality. Every family role helps us develop different strengths.

9. PRONUNCIATION – Word stress in adjectives

Now, let’s improve pronunciation. Some adjectives have two or more syllables. In English, one syllable is stressed more strongly than the others.

Listen to these words and repeat. I’ll say them slowly, marking the stress:

amˈbitious – ambicioso,
reˈbellious – rebelde,
reˈsponsible – responsable,
ˈsensible – sensato,
unˈfriendly – antipático,
imˈpatient – impaciente.

Very good! Notice the pattern — when we add prefixes like un- or im-, the stress usually stays in the same place. For example:
ˈfriendly → unˈfriendly,
ˈpatient → imˈpatient.

Let’s practice in sentences:
  • My sister is ambitious – Mi hermana es ambiciosa.
  • He’s rebellious, but he’s also creative – Es rebelde, pero también creativo.
  • She’s very sensible about money – Es muy sensata con el dinero.

Great! Paying attention to word stress makes your English sound clearer and more natural.

So, we’ve talked about how families influence who we are and how to describe personality traits in English.

Families are full of fun, love… and sometimes, a few problems! Now, we’re going to listen to two short stories about brothers and sisters — James and Marilyn.

Yes, these stories show how siblings sometimes fight or do something they never forget! Listen carefully and imagine the situation.

James says:

What amazing stories! Everyone has childhood memories like these — moments of laughter, trouble, or learning.

Definitely! Let’s talk about them.

That’s true. Now it’s your turn! Think of a time when you were young and you did something funny, silly, or even a bit naughty.

Use the past simple to tell your story. For example:

I can imagine! Remember to use regular and irregular verbs correctly.

went, saw, ate are irregular.

For example:

11. WRITING – A self-portrait poem

Let’s finish with a fun writing task. You’ll create a short poem about yourself — a “self-portrait.”

Here’s the structure. Listen and then write your version later.

1 – Write your first name.
2 – Add four adjectives that describe you.
3 – Write your position in your family and your parents’ names.
4 – Write three things you like.
5 – Write something you’re afraid of.
6 – Write something you’d like to do.
7 – Finally, write your last name.

For example:

Anna
Friendly, creative, patient, and curious
Oldest daughter of Robert and Claire
Loves teaching English, books, and long walks
Afraid of big spiders
Would like to visit Japan one day
Williams

That’s a wonderful example! It’s simple, but it helps you express who you are and practice descriptive language in English.
